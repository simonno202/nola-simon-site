People hear the word "futurist" and assume it means someone who predicts things. That I have a model, a forecast, a confident answer about what 2030 looks like.

I don't. Nobody does.

The future isn't predictable. It never was. What changes isn't the uncertainty — that's always been there. What changes is your relationship to it.

That's the whole point of Everyday Futurism. It's not a forecasting methodology. It's a practice. Something you do in small, daily increments that builds your capacity to move when things shift — before the path is obvious, before permission is granted, before everyone else has noticed what you've already been seeing.

Here's what I've found to be true: most people aren't stuck because they lack information. They're stuck because they've been trained to wait. Wait for more data. Wait for confirmation. Wait until the picture is clearer. But the picture is never fully clear. You're always moving into uncertainty. The only question is whether you've practiced it enough to move anyway.

Prediction is a crutch. It gives you the illusion of control. If I can forecast what's coming, I can prepare. But the kind of preparation prediction creates is brittle — it only works if the prediction is right. And it usually isn't, not exactly, not at the right time, not in the right form.

Practice is different. Practice builds something sturdier. It's not about getting the future right. It's about getting yourself ready for futures you haven't imagined yet.

What does the practice look like? Honestly, it looks boring from the outside. It's noticing signals before they're trends. It's sitting with discomfort instead of resolving it too quickly. It's making one small, honest move before you feel ready. It's building self-trust — not confidence as a mood, but trust as a habit, a track record you've built with yourself through repeated small acts of following what you actually see.

That's the tagline I use: the future isn't predicted, it's practiced. It's not a slogan. It's a structural description of how real change happens. Not through the person who called the shot, but through the person who kept moving when the shot was wrong.

You don't need to be a futurist to do this. You need to stop waiting for one.
